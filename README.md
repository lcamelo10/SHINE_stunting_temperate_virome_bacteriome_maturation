# Temperate phage microdiversity reflects infant gut microbiome maturation independent of chronic undernutrition 

Here you can find all the data and code associated to my [pre-print]() 

## Project Conceptualization

- **Overarching vision:** To understand early-life gut temperate virome and bacteriome assembly during early life and the effects of stunting.
- **Aim:** Determine if stunting (chronic malnutrition) alters bacterial and temperate phage maturation trajectories, providing insights for researchers on (gut) microbial ecology and/or public health focused on improving infant health outcomes.

## About

This study uses bulk metagenomic data from healthy and stunted infants in Zimbabwe from the [SHINE]() cohort to characterize the temperate phage and bacterial communities maturation. We described the maturation at the abundance (macrodiversity) and nucleotide diversity (microdiversity) levels.

We found that: 
- The infant gut bacteriome and virome undergo an age-dependent succession process (maturation) in the first 18 months of life, transitioning from early colonizers to more diverse communities.
- While wasting (or severe acute malnutrition) is known to delay the bacterial maturation, the impact of stunting (a more prevalent condition) remained unclear. Here, we evidenced that stunting does not have an effect on either of the communities.
- Standard studies focus on species-level abundance patterns (macrodiversity), here we show that viral genomic variation (microdiversity) captures maturation and serves as another maturarrion layer independent from abundance and composition.
